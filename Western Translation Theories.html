Western Translation Theories

    There are two critical turns in the development of translation studies: the linguistic turn and the cultural turn. In recent years, there's a growing interest of scholars to apply sociologist theories to the investigation of the translation phenomenon,such as Pierre Bourdieu's theory and Latour's actor-network theory. The following list shows some of the representative western translation theories in chronological order.


     1900s-1930s:
Walter Benjamin——The deconstructionist approach——The Task of Translator
     1940s-1950s:
Roman Jakobson——The Prague school——Interlingual,intralingual and intersemiotic translation
     1960s-1970s:
Eugene Nida——The linguistic approach——Dynamic/formal equivalence
J.C.Catford——The linguistic approach——Translation shift (level&category)
George Steiner——The philosophical approach——The hermeneutical motions of translation:initial trust,aggression,incorporation,compensation
Katharina Reiss——The functional approach——Text type（informative,expressive,operative）
Itamar Even-Zohar——The descriptive approach——The polysystem theory
Gideon Toury——The descriptive approach——Translation norms
     1980s:
Hans Vermeer——The functional approach——The Skopos theory
Peter Newmark——The linguistic approach——Communicative/semantic translation
     1990s—:
Christiane Nord——The functional approach——function plus loyalty
Lawrence Venuti——The cultural approach——Foreignization
Sherry Simon——The cultural approach——Translation and gender
Tjaswini Niranjana——The cultural approach——Postcolonial translation theory

【Reference: Munday, J. (2016). Introducing Translation Studies: Theories and Applications (4th Edition). Routledge.】
